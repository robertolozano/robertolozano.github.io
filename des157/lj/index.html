Game Design UX Best Practices

April 16, 2019

Amir Dori

G ames have become a very normal part of our lives and things such as menus, popups, or button placement aren't really things that one considers too important. However, in their article "Game Design UX Best Practices", Amir Dori, goes on to share his vast experience in game design, after having designed 250+ games and shares how extremely important these elements are and all the thought that goes into it. Amir covers a wide array of topics such as store dialog, rate us dialogs, draggable objects, sliders, menus, and positioning of elements on the screen. However, the common theme that stood out to me which really surprised me because I had not really noticed until now was that the buttons placed in more comfortable and easy to reach zones of the screen are the ones that benefit the app most, such as stores, rating, or ads. Since these are placed in easier to reach locations then they are more likely to be clicked on. From this, you can see how the placement is very intentional and not by accident at all. Amir also points out the way in which this skirts the line of being dark UI, however, Amir also gives good advice on making sure that the transactions are a win-win situation such as making sure the video rewards are actually useful to the player, thus never completely taking advantage of the user.

10 Intriguing Photographs to Teach Close Reading and Visual Thinking Skills

February 27, 2015

Michael Gonchar

C uriosity is powerful. This is something that Gonchar drives home in their article "10 Intriguing Photographs to Teach Close Reading and Visual Thinking Skills", where Gonchar lays out the importance of digging deeper and exploring all the tiny details. By doing this, one is able to learn a lot more about the world and extrapolate information that they would have otherwise missed. Thus Gonchar urges the reader to be curious and always be seeking out new ideas and expanding their view of the world by examining all the little details. A site that I feel exemplifies this curiosity is the site by designer Virgil Abloh, public---domain. On his site, he doesn't follow your typical layout for a website. Much like his designs, Virgil decides to break what is considered the norm and decides to take his own spin on things. Instead it's set up like a computer's desktop with all sorts of information being presented to you. You have all sorts of windows open ranging from an interview to an image that links you to an article on Aya Brown. Furthermore, since the website is set up like a desktop you can also explore the files on the computer, which invites you to just explore through the wide array of images, videos, and files. Through that journey of discovery, you get to take a peek into the early designs or inspirations of some of Virgil's iconic pieces and slowly understand perhaps what Virgil and his team were thinking while creating his work. This I feel truly shows that curiosity and willingness to dig deeper to expand your world knowledge that Gonchar was speaking upon.

Best Practices for Modals / Overlays / Dialog Windows

March 20, 2017

Naema Baskanderi

M odals have been a long-standing staple of the internet world. We've all become very accustomed to them. However, our familiarity with modals doesn't always come with a positive connotation. This is a key part of what the author, Naema Baskanderi, addresses in their article Best Practices for Modals / Overlays / Dialog Windows . Here, Baskanderi addresses the key flaws that we often see with modals, which is the overuse in abruptly interrupting the user experience to bombard you with popup-like information. As users, we have become too accustomed to dismissing them immediately when the entire purpose of the modals should be to give us useful information or help us accomplish a task.

I myself, relate to a lot of the points that Baskanderi brings up, as I've experienced lots of poor uses of modals. However, I feel like I have learned a lot from this article in how I can improve my use of modals in the future. One important point that is really driven home throughout the article is to avoid interrupting the user. Everything must cater to the user experience and must come from the user's intention. Some examples of this that are brought up in the article are to ensure the modals are user-initiated and making your buttons/title as helpful to the user so that they can accomplish the goal they were trying to accomplish. The one that stood out to me was making sure the modal was escapable by keyboard which is something I had not considered but can see how it would drastically improve accessibility for users. Overall, I feel I have learned a lot from the article which I will be implementing in my modals moving forward.

Best practices for form design

April 14, 2021

Salim Ansari

T he article, "Best practices for form design" by Salim Ansari, goes into 20 tips to boost form usability. The tips cover a range of topics, ranging from input fields, placeholder text, and dropdown menus. Among all of these, the most common theme I saw was that often "less is more".

The tips that stood out to me were actually some that weren't completely obvious to me initially. For example, Ansari recommends that fields should be sized according to the input. My natural instinct would be to make all fields the same size in order to keep everything symmetrical. However, after reading Ansari's argument and looking at common websites that I use, I realized that fields such as zipcodes are easier to identify since I instinctively know that the field requires less characters.

The other tip that stood out to me that I have also found myself doing often, is having crucial info as a placeholder text. Ansari points out that having crucial info as a placeholder creates a dilemma where the user can no longer see what the useful info is when they are typing and may have to delete the text in order to see the tip again which creates a lesser user experience.

Overall I feel that after reading Ansari's article, I have gained a lot of useful knowledge on how to improve the user experience for forms. As someone who has spent some time making websites, I have never taken into account all that goes into making good forms. After this, I will ensure that my forms are simpler and easier to navigate.

A website that I use very often is the clothing shop COS. It incorporates many of these useful tips in their checkout form, which has appropriately sized input fields, crucial info below the input fields and not as placeholders, can easily tab between input fields, a progress bar to see how far along the form you are, and many of these other tips.
